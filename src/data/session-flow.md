# Session Flow

## Overview

A Cards Against Bureaucracy session moves through five phases: Challenge, Deliberation, Dialogue, and Synthesis. Each phase has a defined purpose, inputs, and outputs. Sessions are designed to be run in 60-90 minutes facilitated, or asynchronously over a longer period.

---
1. **The Challenge** — The user presents a real organizational challenge by completing a form based on relevant worksheets. This is the central question and context the session will examine. 
The user:
- States the challenge clearly and without bias
- Names the key stakeholders and what is at stake
- Provides any relevant context (organizational history, urgency, constraints)
**Inputs:** User's description of the challenge and context
**Outputs:**  Challenge statement finalized. A clear, shared understanding of the challenge that all personas will respond to.

**Format:**
Challenge: [one clear statement of the challenge]
Stakeholders: [who is affected and how]
Context: [relevant organizational history or constraints]
Stakes: [what happens if this is resolved well vs. poorly]

2. **The Draw** — The user draws one card from each suit. Cards surface specific concepts, tensions, frameworks, or tools relevant to the challenge. The order of the draw is: Barrier, Enabler, Theory, Tool, Provocation.

3. **The Deliberation** — Each persona interprets the spread, offering their perspective, insights, and concerns.
**Order:** Personas respond in the following sequence:
**Critic** — Names what stands in the way
**Optimist** — Names what can be leveraged
**Academic** — Situates the challenge in evidence and theory 
**Practitioner** — Names what can be done
**Philosopher** — Challenges the framing

**Output:** Five distinct perspectives on the challenge

4. **The Dialogue** — Personas respond to one another, building on agreements, challenging contradictions, and exploring tensions.

**Format:**
- Each persona may respond to one or two contributions from other personas
- Responses surface points of agreement, productive disagreement, or new questions
- The Lead may ask clarifying questions or invite specific exchanges ("Practitioner, how do you respond to the Critic's point about incentive structures?")

**Dialogue should:**
- Deepen the deliberation rather than repeat it
- Surface tensions that are productive rather than circular
- Allow the Philosopher's provocations to unsettle and reframe
- Allow the Critic and Optimist to challenge each other constructively

5. **The Synthesis** — The Lead synthesizes the deliberation into a structured output: key insights, recommended actions, risks to monitor, and open questions.

**The Lead:**
- Recaps the challenge as understood after deliberation (it may have shifted)
- Names the 3-5 most significant insights from the deliberation
- Identifies tensions that remain unresolved
- Recommends 3-5 concrete next steps
- Flags risks to monitor
- Names open questions worth carrying forward

**Output format:**

## Challenge Recap
[Restated challenge after deliberation — note any reframing]

## Key Insights
1. [Insight]
2. [Insight]
3. [Insight]

## Recommended Actions
1. [Specific, actionable step]
2. [Specific, actionable step]
3. [Specific, actionable step]

## Risks to Monitor
- [Risk]
- [Risk]
- [Risk]

## Open Questions
- [Question worth carrying forward]
- [Question worth carrying forward]
- [Question worth carrying forward]

**Output:** A richer, more textured understanding of the challenge with key tensions named.
